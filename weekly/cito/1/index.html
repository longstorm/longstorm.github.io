  • physical calculations

    (text by Alan Eliasen from futureboy.us/frinkdocs) 

    In the movie Independence Day, the alien mother ship is said to be 500 km in diameter and have a mass 1/4 that of earth's moon. If the mother ship were a sphere, what would its density be? (The volume of a sphere is 4/3 pi radius3)

    (-> (fj_ (fj 1/4 :moonmass)
             (fj 4/3 :pi)
             (fj** (fj 500/2 :km) 3))
        (to :water) str)

    ;; "280.68438439732194 [dimensionless]"”

    This makes the ship 280 times denser than water. This is 36 times denser than iron and more than 12 times denser than any known element! As the ship is actually more a thin disc than a sphere, it would actually be even denser. Since it contains lots of empty space, parts of it would have to be much, much denser.

    If the object is this dense and has such a large mass, what is its surface gravity? Surface gravity is given by G mass / radius2, where G is the gravitational constant:

    (-> (fj_ (fj :G 1/4 :moonmass)
             (fj** (fj 500/2 :km) 2))
        (to :gravity) str)

    "2.000331549387406 [dimensionless]"”

    The surface gravity of the spaceship is thus at least twice earth's gravity-- and that's on the rim where gravity is weakest. It would actually be much higher since it's much, much flatter than a sphere. I hope you're not the alien that has to go outside and paint it.

    martintrojer/frinj

    The calculations above and many more were written by Alan Eliasen. He created frink, a  “practical calculating tool and programming language designed to make physical calculations simple”, and the little fj bits above are from Martin Trojer's frinj, a Clojure and ClojureScript DSL inspired by frink. In the examples directory of the frinj repository you’ll find more physical diversions. 

    Now how nice would it be to have a Wolfram Alpha-like toy of an interface on a browser, using frinj? Since it's already running in cljs (on Node.js), it should be easy enough for anyone, say, proficient or learning Om to make an example web app.

  • apartment floors

    “Baker, Cooper, Fletcher, Miller, and Smith live on the first five floors of this apartment house. Baker does not live on the fifth floor. Cooper does not live on the first floor. Fletcher does not live on either the fifth or the first floor. Miller lives on a higher floor than does Cooper. Smith does not live on a floor adjacent to Fletcher's. Fletcher does not live on a floor adjacent to Cooper's.”

    —The Art of the Propagator
    tgk/propaganda

    How would you go about solving a problem where you might have to make assumptions, explore options, and possibly back-track frequently to try the alternative? One answer is implicit-search, explored in the Scheme language by Sussman and Radul in their MIT paper The Art of the Propagator, which describes a declarative, constraint-based programming paradigm.

    Thomas Kristensen’s propaganda is a clj and cljs library implementing the propagator model. It still lacks, however, the ability to hold alternate worldviews as proposed in Subsection 6.2 of the original paper, which is what would allow us to easily solve things like the apartment floors problem above .
