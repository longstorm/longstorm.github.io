Clojure in the Open is a weekly newsletter bringing interesting and simple tasks from small to medium open source projects that anyone can contribute to.

12 tasks are featured this week.
29decibel/assistant

Assistant, as the name implies, helps you get to what you need done faster, like a hubot or launcher program (e.g. Quicksilver, Launchy, Alfred), only this time with a rich cards interface powered by ClojureScript's Om.

It’s very hackable and in fact needs a more formal manner to declare its extensions, by allowing them to be fetched from GitHub repos listed in a config file, presumably through a lein plugin during the build process, instead of needing extensions to be part of the Assistant repository as they are now.

skatenerd/meinsweeper

Here's a simple enough game, both for playing and contributing. One especially fun thing about it is that it’s heavily built on core.logic, and below is just a sampler of tasks you can get involved with. If you find them too simple, there are some cool AI open issues to tackle.

A mine sweeper game is a simple thing, especially visually. Concoct a GUI for it, for it has none. You can use whatever library feels best..

Skinney/coc-helper

People are doing some really unique things out in the open with Clojure web development, like this full-stack app helper and tracker for the pen & paper Call of Cthulhu RPG. Below are a couple equally unique things you can help out with, if either Reagent or nightmare-inducing entities are your cup of tea: 

Weapons for each character are currently stored as a hash-map, which unfortunately prevents use-cases such as dual-wielding a pair of a given weapon. The weapons’ data shape needs a rethink.

Alert triggers for stats, some Lovecraftian examples being: lose 1/3 of HP and you're unconscious, lose 5 SAN at once and you’re temporarily insane, lose 1/3 SAN in a game hour and you're indefinitely insane. Sounds fun.

rm-hull/big-bang

Inspired by Racket's big-bang, this eponymous library is a main-loop, world-state functional abstraction very suitable for animation, games and more generally as an actor-based system, where independent processes tick away at their tasks while communicating through core.async.

Below you can see a master big-bang broadcasting digits every second to 6 worker big-bangs who do the job of tweening their old digit to the new one, Bezier-style:

Here's the full source to the visualization above.
The messaging protocol is currently a simple Clojure map, and that offers some limitations and potential perils. If it’s upgraded to an actual record-based implementation, it will also make it much easier to implement, for instance, rewinding and fast-forwarding time and state.
